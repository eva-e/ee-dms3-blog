On Monday of week 3 we focused on Continuity Editing and Formal Continuity. Firstly we began by sharing our chosen video for the Editing Prompt Task which we received the week before. After, we went through theory on both Continuity Editing and Formal Continuity. Continuity Editing is driven by narrative shots, which enables the audience to assume or predict the next shot, maintaining consistency with shots also. The styles of Continuity Editing include, Eyeline Match, Match on Action, Establishing Shot, POV Shot, Axis of Angle, Close-up/Extreme Close-up, Mid Shot, Wide Shot and Parallel Editing/Cross-cutting. Additionally we went through the Formal Continuity styles, which use the elements of speed, duration, lighting and colour, to bring the shots into relationship with each other, which forms meaning for the audience, rather than narrative driven shots. Formal Continuity styles include, Graphical, Aesthetic. Rhythm/Movement, Tempo and Spatial.
In regards to my Editing Prompt which I shared on Monday, I chose The Wind Rises, a Studio Ghibli Film. The prompt was:
Annotate a 2 minute piece of video. It can be anything: video art, a segment of a film or television show, an advert, something online - any piece of moving image with cuts in it. Take note of the following: - the number and frequency of the cuts - the duration between the cuts - what purpose each cut serves - are transitions used or are they hard cuts? - how is time and space established via the cuts? - what are the relationship between shots - graphical, compositional, aesthetic etc - how does sound work with or against the cuts?

(https://youtu.be/eJ3WYCZuKbAA) A Studio Ghibli Film - The Wind Rises by Hayao Miyazaki.

The duration between the cuts was quite short and featured around 21 hard cuts with many quick cuts. There weren't any transitions included, as the focus was on the interaction between the two main characters. Each cut served to portray each character's role in this scene. The scene depicts the power of wind, able to make the paths of two strangers meet.

The first few cuts reveal and somewhat foreshadow that the man's hat will fly away. It jumps from the woman to the man, signalling to the audience that there is an importance in their meeting. The purpose of the first several cuts is to establish the space between the characters and bring our attention to the very moment in front of us, as the scene does not change time or space and we are limited to the back of two train carriages.

Many aesthetic cuts are added here which we can see by the book, the bridge below the train, the couplers holding each carriage and the hat being taken by the wind. These cuts focus away from the characters and underpin the objects of the scene which lead to the event of the characters meeting. All these aspects within the scene build the narrative of their interaction from beginning to end, though we are left eager to know when they will meet again.

In 5 seconds of a part of the clip, there are 6 cuts, which displays how quickly the wind changes their situations, from enjoying the wind and reading a novel, to a dangerous encounter with death and rescuing a stranger's hat. The cuts here begin from the rapid speed of the train going over a bridge, the turning of the pages and finally his hat flying away. As rapid cuts are being shown, the sound also plays a part by increasing in volume, intensifying the scene, a slight warning to the audience that something is about to happen. It also indicates that a strong wind has risen, an indirect reference to the title, The Wind Rises.

After their conversation there is a cut to the couplers which connect each carriage, once again making us aware that while separate, on different sides of the train, they are still joined together, hinting at the future of their relationship and possible second encounter. These scene cuts emphasise that while separated, there is still a connection present, a strong bond between each carriage and a bridge between them that may be crossed again, which evidently happens later in the film. The last cut is a wide shot, a third person view of the train travelling past fields of green. The scene embodies the main title and the power of wind in a poetic way.

Furthermore, on Wednesday I participated in another group task which was a practical, shooting to edit. We were allocated into groups of 3 or 4. Part of the task was to choose 5 Continuity Editing styles for our video. Our group chose, POV, Close-up, Wide Shot, Establishing Shot and Match on Action.

The concept was on a student discovering posters in RMIT, however they were alone. We used Close-up shots to encapsulate the students emotions of both sadness and longing, especially how the light reflects in their eyes as they gaze upon the posters. We also included multiple Match on Action shots which displayed the continuous journey of the student, leaving one scene and picking up where they left off in another, from the hallway, to the staircase and finally at the wall of posters. It may have seemed like the staircase led to that wall of posters however those two scenes were not a continuation of the same area, it was the continuation of the shot however at a different location. While this may not seem consistent, we attempted to create consistency by implying the student had arrived at the top of the same staircase and encountered the wall of posters. The staircase was in Building 13 level 1 and the wall of posters in Building 9 level 1 staircase. The soundtrack is a non-copyright song from Youtube, called Hopeful by Cool Cinema.
